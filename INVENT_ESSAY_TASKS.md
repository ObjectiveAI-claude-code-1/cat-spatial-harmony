1. Evaluate the purposefulness of negative space. Look at the areas of the photograph that are not occupied by the cat or by prominent objects and determine whether that empty space feels intentional and framing, or accidental and neglectful. Purposeful negative space cradles the cat — it gives the subject room to breathe, creates a sense of calm and intentionality, and makes the viewer feel that the world within the frame has made space for the cat to exist comfortably. Poor negative space either abandons the cat in a vast, meaningless void that makes it feel small and forgotten, or crowds the cat against the edges of the frame as though the photograph is closing in on it. The task is to judge whether the emptiness around the cat is serving the cat — staging it like silence stages music — or whether it is ignoring the cat entirely.

2. Evaluate the coherence of background elements. Look at the objects, textures, colors, and shapes that exist behind and around the cat and determine whether they feel like they belong to the same visual story as the subject. A coherent background collaborates with the cat — its elements tell a unified story and collectively guide the viewer's eye back to the subject, regardless of whether the background is simple or richly detailed. An incoherent background competes with the cat — dominant shapes pull the eye away, clashing colors fight with the cat's fur, aggressive textures flatten the subject into the scenery, and the photograph becomes a visual search puzzle rather than a portrait. The task is to judge whether the background elements are working as a supportive ensemble cast that enhances the cat's presence, or whether they have forgotten whose portrait this is.

3. Evaluate the proportional presence of the subject. Look at the cat within the full frame of the photograph and determine whether it occupies its space with appropriate stature and dignity. Proportional presence means the cat feels like the protagonist of its own photograph — it is anchored in the image, grounded in its environment, and given enough room to be itself without being swallowed by surroundings that dwarf it. This is not strictly about how large the cat appears; a small cat in a vast landscape can feel powerful if the composition grants it visual weight and centrality. The task is to judge whether the cat feels like it belongs in and commands the space it inhabits — neither cramped and constricted by a frame pressing too tightly against it, nor diminished into a minor detail lost within an environment that has overtaken the portrait.
